1.  What will be considered to donate blood?

You are eligible to donate blood if:

  1. Have a healthy body
  2. You are aged:
    1. Between 18 to 65 years for those who have donated before
    2. Between 18 to 60 years for those to donated the first time
    3. 17 years old and below is allowed with written permission from parents/guardian
  3. Weight 45kg and above
  4. Sleep a minimum of 5 hours
  5. No health problems
  6. Have taken food before donating blood
  7. Not engage in any high risk activities as follows:
    1. Same -sex relationships (homosexual)
    2. Gender relationship with both sexes (bisexual)
    3. Sex with commercial sex workers
    4. Exchanging sex partners
    5. Taking drugs by injection
    6. Become a partner of the type to the above group
  8. Last donation 3 months ago
  9. For female donors, make sure you are NOT:
    1. Pregnant
    2. Menstruating
    3. Breastfeeding
  10. Do not donate if you have:
    1. Lived in United Kindom (England, Northern, Ireland, Scotlaand, Wales, Isle of Man or Channel Island) or the Republic of Ireland from 1980 to 1996 for a period of 6 months (cumulative) or more
    2. Have lived in Europe from 1980 to now for a period of 5 years (accumulated) or more
  11. The following ORIGINAL documents are needed for donating blood:

    Malaysian Foreigner
    Civil / Military / Police Identity Card Passport
    Driving license Work Permit with Passport No. and photo
    Employee / Student Card with Identity Card No. and photo Student Card with Passport No. and photo

    *Only original documents are accepted as identification. Foreigners living more than 1 year in Malaysia are ONLY ALLOWED for blood donation. PATI, UNHCR and foreigners unable to read and understand Bahasa Malaysia or English are not allowed to donate (translators are not allowed).

2.  What is the process to donate blood?

Each individual who donates blood with the National Blood Center (PDN) will go through the donation process as follows:

  1. Print out their details from Life4U 'MyProfile' portal.
  2. Undergo a blood test for red blood cell concentration levels (hemoglobin levels) and identify ABO blood groups
  3. Go through questionnaire sessions or pre-donation counseling to identify any medical problems and donor risk levels
  4. Eligible donors will undergo a blood collection process (phlebotomy)
  5. Upon completion of the blood collection process, the donor will be given some food and soft drinks

3.  What need to be done after donating blood?

Each time after donating blood, you are advised to pay attention to the following:

  1. Press firmly on the needle prick container while resting for at least 10 minutes in bed before getting up
  2. Take some of the drinks and snacks provided before leaving the donation site
  3. Take one tablet of iron and folic acid supplied once a day after meals to help you rebuild the red blood cells you have donated
  4. Be careful while driving or while working with machines
  5. Within 24 hours after donating:
    1. Avoid strenuous sports or exercise activities, or lifting heavy objects
    2. Avoid alcohol
    3. Drink more water
    4. If you experience any reactions or reactions such as dizziness or fainting, lie down for a few minutes with both legs raised above head level
    5. If you feel unwell / uncomfortable, or there are signs of bruising, swelling or pain at the injected area, please contact the National Blood Center Medical Officer immediately at 03-2693 3888 / 03-2695 5564

4.  What type of donations are available?

Donation is not only focused on red blood, but there are other components in the blood that can be donated including:

'Whole blood' donation
Blood contains the following components:

  1. Red blood cells
  2. White blood cells
  3. Platelet
  4. Plasma
Blood is needed for surgical cases, accident victims, treating patients with leukemia, cancer, thalassemia, heart disease, complications of bleeding during childbirth, haemophilia and others. The volume of blood that is usually donated is 450ml which is less than 1 pint of blood (568ml). The amount of blood taken is based on weight.

The time taken to donate blood is between 7 to 10 minutes but you are advised to be at the donation site for about 1 hour. Before donating blood you are advised to take some food as a stomach pad. Donated red blood takes 4 - 8 weeks to be replaced while plasma takes 24 hours.

Apheresis donation
Donation of blood components such as plasma or platelets or both, where unnecessary blood components are returned to the donor's body.

5.  How the blood would be stored and used?

Donated blood can be divided into several types of products or components:

  • Red blood cells:   Whole blood, packed red cells, buffy-coat poor red cells, filtered red cells
  • Plasma:   Fresh frozen plasma, cryosupernatant, cryoprecipitate
  • Platelet:  Random platelet concentrate, platelet apheresis

Each of these blood products or components has a different way of storage and use. Among them:
  • Blood that has gone through a screening test and has been processed into red blood cells, platelets and plasma will be stored according to blood type, ABO blood group and expiration date respectively
  • Red blood cells need to be stored at a temperature of 2oC - 6oC and can survive for 35 days
  • Plasma should be stored at -30oC and can last for 2 years
  • Platelets should be stored at a temperature of 20oc - 24oC and should be constantly agitated. The shelf life of platelets is only 5 days from the date of donation
Each of these blood products or components has its own uses:
  • Whole blood:   A unit of donated blood that does not undergo the process of isolation. Used for cases such as patients who lost blood due to an accident and for blood exchange (Exchange Transfusion, ET) of infants due to severe neonatal jaundice
  • Red blood cells:   For hemoglobin deficiency (anemia) such as kidney disease and cancer
  • Plasma:   Cases of bleeding during childbirth, burns due to fire, bleeding due to liver damage
  • Platelet:  For platelet deficiency diseases such as dengue fever and leukemia

8.  What is the medical benefit of blood donation?

Blood donation is not only beneficial for the society but also helps the donor to maintain good health. The health benefits of donating blood are mentioned below:

  • Prevents Hemochromatosis
    • Health benefits of blood donation include reduced risk of hemochromatosis. Hemochromatosis is a health condition that arises due to excess absorption of iron by the body. This may be inherited or may be caused due to alcoholism, anemia or other disorders. Regular blood donation may help in reducing iron overload. Make sure that the donor meets the standard blood donation eligibility criteria.

  • Anti-Cancer Benefits
    • Blood donation helps in lowering the risk of cancer. By donating blood the iron stores in the body are maintained at healthy levels. A reduction in the iron level in the body is linked with low cancer risk.

  • Maintains Healthy Heart & Liver
    • Blood donation is beneficial in reducing the risk of heart and liver ailments caused by the iron overload in the body. Intake of iron-rich diet may increase the iron levels in the body, and since only limited proportions can be absorbed, excess iron gets stored in heart, liver, and pancreas. This, in turn, increases the risk of cirrhosis, liver failure, damage to the pancreas, and heart abnormalities like irregular heart rhythms. Blood donation helps in maintaining the iron levels and reduces the risk of various health ailments.

  • Weight Loss
    • Regular blood donation reduces the weight of the donors. This is helpful to those who are obese and are at higher risk of cardiovascular diseases and other health disorders. However, blood donation should not be very frequent and you may consult your doctor before donating blood to avoid any health issues.

  • Stimulates Blood Cell Production
    • After donating blood, the body works to replenish the blood loss. This stimulates the production of new blood cells and in turn, helps in maintaining good health.

9.  What guidelines need to be followed to organize a blood donation campaign?

The National Blood Center (PDN) is constantly striving to increase the amount of blood collected to be supplied to hospitals that need blood. Therefore, many blood donation programs are planned and implemented by PDN. In order to organize a successful and effective blood donation program, a few things need to be considered:

  1. The minimum number of donors is:
    1. 40 people minimum for static location
    2. 50 mothers for NBB Bus

  2. Donation Place
    3. Size of 30 feet x 30 feet minimum (depending on the number of donors) Comfortable, air -conditioned or fanned, bright, has good air circulation and away from noise interference. Avoid hot, hidden, dusty places and those that need to climb high stairs. Features parking and unloading facilities.

  3. Equipment
    4. Equipment used for the blood donation process will be brought by the PDN. Refreshment after donation will be provided by PDN.

  4. The organizers are requested to provide:
    5. At least 8 tables and 20 chairs for the use of donors and staff on duty (depending on the number of donors) Fans-if the place provided is not air-conditioned or open. Electricity and clean water supply.

  5. Publicity

  6. Posters and pamphlets
    7. for the publicity of this blood donation campaign will be provided by the PDN. The organizers are recommended to announce to the public about the blood donation campaign that will be conducted using the poster provided at least a week before the campaign date. On the day of the donation, an announcement orally or with the help of a public notification system (PA system) can be made to remind the public about the ongoing campaign.
